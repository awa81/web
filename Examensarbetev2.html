Introduction

In today's digital era, a school like Kanalskolan must provide a reliable and efficient Wi-Fi network to support learning, teaching, and administration. As the school prepares to reopen in 2023 after its closure in 2020, there is a need to upgrade and optimize the existing Wi-Fi infrastructure to meet the new requirements of students and teachers. That assignment has been given to CGI, a global lT consulting firm.

This master thesis will initially focus on a greenfield project for Kanalskolan. Greenfield means that I will construct an entirely new WiFi network from scratch, where I am free to choose the type of network equipment I want, where they should be placed, and how many devices I need.

In the next part, a brownfield project will be addressed. Brownfield means that I will be upgrading or expanding an already existing Wi-Fi network.

Finally, I will make a comparison of my greenfield and brownfield solutions for the Wi-Fi network at Kanalskolan. This master thesis will focus on the various difficulties and problems that can arise with the different project approaches, as well as their benefits

3. Theory

3.1 Wi-Fi Standards and Protocols

Overview of the IEEE 802.11 Standard

The original 802.11 standard, published in 1997, focused on the Physical and MAC sublayers. It defined three specifications: Infrared (IR), Frequency Hopping Spread Spectrum (FHSS), and Direct Sequence Spread Spectrum (DSSS). Both FHSS and DSSS operated in the 2.4 GHz ISM band but couldn't communicate with each other. The standard offered 1 Mbps and 2 Mbps data rates, with actual throughput being considerably lower. Modern WLAN deployments use newer 802.11 amendments.

IEEE 802.11 Draft Amendments

IEEE 802.11n: Introduced in 2009, the 802.11n standard (also known as Wi-Fi 4) is a significant improvement over previous standards. It supports both 2.4 GHz and 5 GHz frequency bands and provides data rates of up to 600 Mbps. With the introduction of Multiple Input Multiple Output (MIMO) technology, this standard significantly increased range and throughput, making it a popular choice for high-performance networks.

IEEE 802.11ac: Released in 2013, the 802.11ac standard (also known as Wi-Fi 5) operates exclusively in the 5 GHz frequency band and offers data rates of up to 6.93 Gbps. This standard also utilizes MIMO technology and introduces features such as beamforming, which directs Wi-Fi signals towards specific devices to improve performance. The 802.11ac standard is widely adopted for its high-speed capabilities and improved efficiency.

IEEE 802.11ax: The latest Wi-Fi standard, 802.11ax (also known as Wi-Fi 6), was released in 2019. It operates in both 2.4 GHz and 5 GHz frequency bands and offers data rates up to 9.6 Gbps. This standard introduces new technologies such as Orthogonal Frequency Division Multiple Access (OFDMA) and Target Wake Time (TWT), which significantly improve network efficiency, particularly in high-density environments.

3.2 Key technologies that can enhance the network performance

OFDMA (Orthogonal Frequency-Division Multiple Access): This technology improves network efficiency by allowing multiple users to share the same channel simultaneously, reducing latency and increasing capacity.

MU-MIMO (Multi-User Multiple Input Multiple Output): MU-MIMO allows APs to communicate with multiple clients at the same time, enhancing the network's ability to handle simultaneous connections and improving overall performance.

Beamforming: This technology directs the Wi-Fi signal towards individual clients, ensuring better signal strength and reduced interference, which is particularly beneficial for streaming applications.

TWT (Target Wake Time): A feature in Wi-Fi 6 and above that allows devices to negotiate when and how often they will wake up to send or receive data, improving network efficiency and reducing power consumption for battery-powered devices.

BSS Coloring: This Wi-Fi 6 technology helps reduce interference from neighboring networks by assigning a color code to each Basic Service Set (BSS), allowing devices to ignore transmissions from different colored BSSs, thus reducing the impact of co-channel interference.

CleanAir: A Cisco technology that identifies and mitigates radio frequency interference, improving overall network performance and reliability.

Airtime Fairness: By allocating equal airtime to each client, this feature ensures that slower devices do not hinder the performance of faster devices, resulting in a more consistent user experience.

3.2 Wireless Network Components

  1. Access Points (APs): Broadcast Wi-Fi signals, acting as a bridge between wired and wireless networks. They allow wireless clients to connect and access the network resources.
  2. Wireless LAN Controller (WLC): Is responsible for configuring and managing multiple APs. It simplifies network administration by providing a single point of control for various settings and monitoring of the wireless network.
  3. Switches: These devices direct data traffic between network components, connecting wired devices like APs, WLC, and routers.

3.4 Brownfield vs. Greenfield

Brownfield and Greenfield Wi-Fi networks refer to different deployment approaches. Brownfield projects involve upgrading or expanding existing network infrastructure, offering cost savings and utilizing established resources, but may face compatibility and integration challenges. Greenfield projects involve creating entirely new networks, providing flexibility and the opportunity to implement the latest technologies.The choice between Brownfield and Greenfield depends on an organization's specific requirements, budget, and existing infrastructure.

3.5 Predictive and Site Surveys

Predictive and Site Surveys are two critical approaches for planning Wi-Fi network deployment. Predictive surveys use simulation software to model the network, allowing for a theoretical understanding of coverage, signal strength, and interference before physical installation. “The goal of a predictive site survey is to determine where to place APs for the best performance throughout the area”,[15] These surveys are cost-effective and time-saving but may lack real-world accuracy. Site surveys, on the other hand, involve on-site testing and measurement of radio frequencies, signal strength, and interference, providing a more accurate representation of the actual environment. Although more time-consuming and costly, site surveys can significantly improve network performance and help identify potential issues before deployment.

3.8 Ekahau Pro Visualizations View

Visualizations in Ekahau Pro are graphical representations that provide a comprehensive view of various aspects of a wireless network. These visualizations use colors and gradients over the layout of a physical location, like a building floor plan, to display different network metrics such as signal strength, interference, noise levels, and more. This makes it easier to understand, analyze, and plan the network by offering a visual guide to where the network is strong or weak, and where improvements might be needed.[21]

  1. Signal Strength: This visualization shows the Received Signal Strength Indicator (RSSI) of the selected set of access points in dBm. It indicates the strength and reach of your wireless signal. Grey spots signify areas where signal strength no longer meets your requirements.
  2. Network Health: A comprehensive overview indicating whether the network meets set requirements across multiple metrics like signal strength, signal-to-noise ratio (SNR), channel overlap, etc. It helps assess if your network is functioning properly.
  3. Network Issues: If the Network Health visualization reveals any issues, this visualization highlights exactly what those problems are, such as signal strength or SNR failures.
  4. Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR): Displays the difference between the signal strength and noise in a given location. A low SNR could lead to dropped connections, packet loss, or low data transfer rates.
  5. Channel Interference: Displays the level of overlapping Wi-Fi signals that could interfere with your network. High interference could lead to slower speeds and connectivity issues.
  6. Number of Access Points (APs): This visualization shows the number of audible access points in a location, necessary for seamless roaming and load balancing.
  7. Throughput: Displays the measured or estimated net throughput per location, giving insights into potential bandwidth issues.
  8. Round-Trip Time (RTT): Measures the latency in the network by sending ICMP request packets to a host and measuring the time taken for a response.
  9. Packet Loss: Shows the number of lost packets during a network test. High packet loss can indicate network congestion, corrupted packets, or faulty hardware/drivers.
  10. Coverage Planning: A support visualization that shows AP coverage in real-time, taking into account walls and other obstacles.
  11. Secondary/Tertiary Signal Strength: These visualizations show the second and third strongest signals at a location, providing a broader view of the network's overall strength.
  12. Spectrum Channel Power: Depicts the measured spectrum results from a channel-by-channel point of view. It measures all signals and energy from the channels, not just Wi-Fi signals.
  13. Channel Utilization: Shows how congested the measured band or individual channels are. High utilization can indicate high Wi-Fi traffic or noise sources.
  14. Interferers: Detects and displays non-Wi-Fi interference from the surveys on the map.
  15. Difference in Signal Strength: Compares the difference in signal strength between Primary and Secondary surveys, useful for monitoring changes over time.
  16. “One thing to note is that we cannot ‘measure’ the airtime utilization as it may be completely different an hour after a survey is done. So the Airtime shown is always the snapshot of the current situation”[25]

3.10 Radio Resource Management (RRM)

RRM is a collection of algorithms used for managing wireless networks. The key algorithms are RF Grouping, FRA, DCA, TPC, and CHDM. RRM operates under a hierarchy of RF Group Name, RF Group leader(s), and RF Neighborhood(s).[9]

  • The Transmit Power Control (TPC) algorithm is used to adjust the transmit power of access points (APs) based on the radio frequency (RF) environment in order to maximize coverage and minimize co-channel interference. TPCv1 and TPCv2 are two versions of the algorithm, with TPCv1 being the default method for power control in RRM. TPCv1 calculates Tx power on a per AP per radio basis for every member of the RF group and relies on a user-tunable setting called RRM Power Threshold to make decisions on an AP's transmit power. TPCv2, on the other hand, calculates the cell boundary between two APs based on measured RF distance between them and optimizes coverage based on that calculation[10]

3. Wall attenuation

Wall attenuation is how Wi-Fi signals are absorbed or weakened when passing through materials, with the degree of signal reduction dependent on the material type and thickness. Measuring wall attenuation accurately is key to understanding Wi-Fi network coverage and requires tools like the Ekahau Sidekick and a survey-running device.

The measurement process involves setting a baseline for Wi-Fi signal strength or Received Signal Strength Indicator (RSSI) using the Sidekick. This device should be positioned at least five meters from the wall to avoid skewed results due to near-field effects. Once the baseline is set, the Sidekick is moved to the other side of the wall, maintaining the same orientation, to measure the RSSI again. The difference between these measurements provides the attenuation value, indicating the impact of the wall material on the Wi-Fi signal.

Every wall and material may impact the signal differently, so it's necessary to repeat this process for several walls to be sure. Also, environmental factors, such as other electronic devices or physical obstructions, can influence the measurements.

The hidden node problem

The hidden node problem is a common issue in wireless networks where two client stations are at opposite ends of an RF coverage cell and they cannot hear each other. This often happens when coverage cells are too large as a result of the access point's radio transmitting at too much power. Poor WLAN design, obstacles such as walls or bookcases, and users moving behind obstacles can also cause the hidden node problem. Users with wireless desktops often place their radio card underneath a metal desk and effectively transform that radio card into an unheard hidden node. The hidden node problem can be addressed by lowering the data rates if you are planning for capacity or by using RTS/CTS if hidden node problems occur in a network planned for coverage. [13]

Request to Send/Clear to Send (RTS/CTS)

RTS/CTS is a technology in wireless networks that helps manage access to the shared medium and reduce collisions caused by hidden nodes. When a device wants to transmit data, it sends an RTS frame to the access point (AP), which responds with a CTS frame. These frames contain information about the transmission duration, notifying other devices in the network not to transmit during that time to avoid collisions[14]

Ping Packet Loss

Ping Packet Loss represents the percentage of ping packets (small packets of data sent to test network connectivity) that fail to reach their destination when sent from your device to a target device on the same network. This metric provides an understanding of the network's reliability and performance. High packet loss can result in disrupted or slow communications, as it often indicates network congestion, faulty hardware, or issues with the Wi-Fi signal strength or quality

Co-Channel Interference (CCI)

“Overlapping coverage cells with overlapping frequencies causes what is known as co-channel interference (CCI)”[20] It can degrade network performance as only one device can transmit at a time on a given channel. To avoid CCI, it's crucial to use non-overlapping channels (like 1, 6, 11 in the 2.4 GHz band) and to carefully design the network for optimal coverage and minimal interference. This includes considering a three-dimensional channel reuse pattern in multi-story buildings.

“In general, CCI is not a major problem until there are numerous devices operating on the same channel”[22]

Adjacent Channel Interference (ACI)

Adjacent Channel Interference (ACI) refers to the disruption caused to a desired signal by neighboring signals in close frequency proximity. This interference is due to imperfect receiver-side filters that allow the neighboring signal to mix with the actual pass band, making it difficult to differentiate the actual signal from a strong adjacent one. Factors that cause ACI include multiple channels operating on similar frequencies and unwanted power emissions from an adjacent channel. ACI can be reduced by implementing efficient filtering, careful channel assignments, and maintaining consistent spacing between two adjacent cells[24]

Roaming oscillation

Also known as the "ping-pong" effect, occurs when a Wi-Fi client device frequently switches, or roams, between two access points (APs). This typically happens when the client is at equal distance from two APs and it can't decide which one has the better signal. The client ends up continuously bouncing back and forth between the two APs

Sticky client

Refers to a device that stays connected to a weak access point (AP) instead of switching to a stronger one.

Sticky client syndrome

A network issue that arises when multiple devices act as sticky clients, hindering overall network performance.

6. Predictive Survey at Kanalskolan

I imported all the blueprint maps of Kanalskolan into Ekahau Pro and drew out the walls, doors and windows in the building. Since I hadn't visited Kanalskolan before my predictive survey, my supervisor and I agreed to classify all exterior walls as thicker concrete walls with an attenuation of 12dB and interior walls as dry walls with an attenuation of 3dB. This is something that will be adjusted later after I have visited Kanalskolan and conducted a site survey.

When we plan a network design, we need to accommodate the Least Capable Most Important Device (LCMID) to ensure a good user experience for all devices, regardless of their capabilities.This is a problem in educational environments because students may bring a lot of different devices on their own.[8]

In most of the areas in Kanalskolan, I set a requirement for a robot vacuum cleaner that only operates on the 2.4GHzband, with an offset of -10 dB. This way I will be able to spot potential problems in Ekahau’s visualization view.

In the Requirements Area I created one large area for each floor, (see Appendix, Image 3:1),  with a robot vacuum cleaner device that only operates on the 2.4GHzband, with an offset of -10 dB. And one area that I will use for the classrooms. (see Appendix, Image 3:2). that will need capacity for 31 devices, and a total bitrate of 95 Mbps.

In my WiFi design approach, the focus is primarily on the 5.0GHz frequency band due to its advantages in terms of higher data rates, less interference, and better channel availability. The 5.0GHz band provides more channels with wider bandwidth options, allowing for improved capacity and faster connections, especially for bandwidth-intensive applications. “Most Wi-Fi designers take a hybrid approach where they design for both 2.4GHz and 5.0GHz, but optimize for 5GHz”[23]

However, it is important not to neglect the 2.4GHz band. Despite its limitations in terms of channel congestion and interference from other devices, the 2.4GHz band still offers its own benefits. It has a longer range and better wall penetration compared to the 5.0GHz band, making it suitable for providing coverage in larger areas and ensuring connectivity for older devices that only support this frequency. Additionally, some IoT devices and certain applications still operate exclusively on the 2.4GHz band.

“If the wireless network is located in a dense urban environment or multi-tenant building, then it is likely there is too many networks to overcome the amount of channel overlap within the 2.4 GHz frequency space”[23] Also, In scenarios requiring enhanced network capacity, as in the classrooms of Kanalskolan, the presence of multiple APs in close proximity will be necessary, and that will cause even more CCI for the 2.4GHz band.

I can try to adjust the transmit power or disable certain 2.4 GHz radios later, to mitigate co-channel interference (CCI).

When deciding on the locations for APs, it is a best practice to avoid placing them in hallways. Positioning APs in hallways can lead to weaker connections and reduced performance inside the classrooms due to signal attenuation caused by walls and other obstacles, and it’s harder to control the spread of the coverage zone in hallways, which increases the risk of interference. But sometimes it’s necessary if your clients request seamless roaming everywhere.

When placing APs, TPC should be kept in mind to ensure that the APs are not too close or too far apart, as this may cause co-channel interference or result in coverage gaps.[9] The TPC settings should be configured on the Wireless LAN Controller (WLC) to match the network requirements and environment.

I decided to set the minimum data rate for the 2.4 GHz band to 12 Mbps and for the 5 GHz band to 18 Mbps. This choice was made to improve network performance. “By forcing the higher data rates, it is easier to force more distributed capacity over the access points.“[12] It also reduces interference and network congestion, leading to better airtime efficiency. Raising the minimum data rate can also prevent the hidden node problem. Which can occur when two client stations are at opposite ends of an RF coverage cell and they cannot hear each other. “This often happens when coverage cells are too large as a result of the access point’s radio transmitting at too much power”[13]

My choice of AP was the Cisco C9120i 2.4GHz + 5.0GHz, I used 65 APs in total for Kanalskolan. I set the TX Power for the 2.4GHz radio to 6dBm and for the 5.0GHz radio I used 12dBm. TX Power was something I tweaked to see how far the AP would reach if RRM decided to raise the TX Power to 17dBm maximum. RRM needed to hear four other APs to make the best possible decision. And that is a challenge for AP that is placed far out in a corner of a building.

I let Ekahau Pro solve the channel planning and used 40MHz channel bonding for the 5.0 GHz band. The main advantage of channel bonding is that it can significantly increase the maximum data rate. For instance, doubling the channel width from 20MHz to 40MHz can theoretically double the data rate [16]. The tradeoff with channel bonding is fewer channels, and when using 80MHz to 160MHz, I only get 5 to 3 channels to my disposal. And at Kanalskolan with multiple floors and classrooms, I got problems with co-channel interference when I tried channel bonding at 80MHz, but I had no problems with 40MHz bonding of channels.

6.2 Summary and reflections of the result

The predictive survey resulted in a strong signal coverage and good secondary coverage across all needed areas in the three buildings. But it’s easy to get good signal coverage when you don’t have any budget restrictions and can put up as many APs as you want.

However, the survey did highlight a few potential areas of concern. Weaknesses in 2.4GHz signal strength were noted in some hallways and open spaces, and there were instances of CCI identified in a few zones. Additionally, issues regarding roaming oscillation and APs' ability to detect signal strength from other APs were raised in certain areas.

But, it's important to remember that predictive surveys are based on simulations, and their accuracy is based on the information I fed into them. Such as maps, building material information, expected client density and type of device usage. These surveys do not account for real-world variables like dynamic interference, actual building materials, and human behavior. As such, they should be used as a rough guide, not an absolute prediction. In practice, they are best used in conjunction with site surveys for more accurate network design.

"A predictive design isn’t final and ready for deployment until you have validated your design"[18] This survey is an important step to avoid "costly and time-consuming redesign processes that would follow a failed deployment. A site survey involves visiting the location to capture WiFi signal data, inspect access point placements, and understand the unique characteristics of the environment. It's important to consider factors like the school's structural layout, including potential obstructions like walls and furniture, as well as the intended use of each area. By conducting a site survey, I can validate the predictive design in the actual environment and fine-tune the setup as needed.

According to a list of the APs at Kanaskolan that I got from my supervisor, there are 28 different APs in the school that CGI controls.

In my measurements I detected 225 different APs and 34 APs with the SKEGUEST network. I was only able to locate 25 of those 34 APs, but there were some areas of the school I was unable to enter due to construction. And some APs might have been detected from surrounding buildings.

During my analyse of the site survey results, I have focused on the 5.0GHz s but I have also constantly checked the 2.4GHz band, looking for potential problem zones or other issues with the network. I gone over most of Ekahau Pro’s visualization views, and chosen the most interesting ones to the (Appendices, Site Survey)

Building 1, Basement (See Appendix, Image 4:1-4:8)

AP-85:80 provided good coverage from the left side of the building, but the signal strength in the teachers' office spaces was insufficient, with no secondary coverage. AP-85:80 could only detect two other APs, impairing RRM's ability to make good decisions.

I also identified potential issues with the 'sticky node' problem, particularly for teachers entering the building from the right side. They could get stuck with AP-4c:b0 on the bottom floor, causing potential roaming oscillation in the office space of the basement. This problem was more pronounced for the 2.4GHz band, leading to potential 'ping-pong' issues.

Furthermore, both the 2.4GHz and 5.0GHz networks experienced packet loss issues, primarily in certain gray areas.

Lastly, the presence of two rogue APs in the teachers' office space was probably something that contributed to channel interference and packet loss, affecting both the 2.4GHz and 5.0GHz bands.

Building 2, Basement (See Appendix, Image 3:8-3:12)

In Building 2's basement, the 5.0GHz SKEGUEST network generally displayed robust signal strength. There were, however, a few areas of concern. Office space 2 exhibited weaker signal strength as it received better signal from an AP above. Secondary coverage was lacking in the office spaces. Additionally, co-channel interference was noted from APs on the floor above, particularly for the 2.4GHz band.

Regarding airtime utilization, inconsistent results in classrooms warrant further investigation. In one classroom, measurements from different APs were recorded depending on the location, an anomaly not explained by signal strength or channel coverage views. These issues could potentially affect user experience and network performance, and may require additional measures such as AP relocation or power adjustments.

Building 1 and Building 2, Bottom Plane (See Appendix, Image 3:13-3:14)

The bottom plane of Building 1 and Building 2 exhibited strong signal strength for the 5.0GHz SKEGUEST network, with optimal coverage even in the teachers' office space. However, issues were identified regarding channel interference. Multiple rogue APs were detected in both buildings, causing considerable co-channel interference, particularly in the gray zones. The presence of several rogue APs, possibly from an adjacent building, intensifies the problem.

Airtime utilization was mostly satisfactory across the area, although one middle classroom had a relatively high utilization of 42%, warranting closer attenuation. Some inconsistencies were found in AP selection based on the measurement location. Additionally, certain zones, especially in the hallways and stairs, show a potential for roaming oscillation, indicating an area for possible improvement. These irregularities may impact the seamless Wi-Fi experience and should be addressed in the network optimization process.

Building 1 and Building 2, First Floor (See Appendix, Image 3:15-3:16)

On the first floor of Building 1 and Building 2, the 2.4GHz and 5.0GHz networks for SKEGUEST showed satisfactory signal strength, with the secondary and tertiary signals also performing well in Building 2. However, there were concerns regarding channel interference. A rogue AP was identified in the hallway of Building 2, predominantly affecting the 2.4GHz network. Additionally, multiple weak rogue AP signals were detected through the hallway windows. This led to considerable channel congestion, especially on the 2.4GHz band, with over ten APs detected on the same channel in certain zones.

Though associated access points performed well for the 5.0GHz network, some issues were noticed in the hallway and classroom for the 2.4GHz band, with potential roaming oscillation in certain spots. Furthermore, airtime utilization was generally good, though some inconsistencies were observed in AP selection based on measurement location. These factors need to be considered during network optimization to ensure reliable connectivity.

Building 1 and Building 2, Second and Third Floors (See Appendix, Image 3:17-3:18)

There were no APs detected in Building 1, which seems to primarily serve as storage space. However, Building 2 had four APs connected to the SKEGUEST network. There was a peculiar case of two APs located very close together in the same room, both using channel 6 on the 2.4GHz band, which might lead to co-channel interference. Also, signal strength was found to be a bit weak in Building 2's office space and one of the classrooms. A notable risk of roaming oscillation was identified in the area with the two closely-located APs, potentially affecting both 2.4GHz and 5.0GHz frequencies. Co-channel interference was reported from the floor below and the hallway windows, suggesting certain channels may need to be avoided.

On the third floor of both buildings, no APs were detected. Signal strength in the potential office space was found to be weak, sourced from the APs on the floor below. While the space isn't currently in use, if it's repurposed as a teachers' office in the future, stronger Wi-Fi coverage will be needed.

Building 3 (See Appendix, Image 3:19-3:22)

In Building 3, measurements could not be taken from the left part of the building due to ongoing construction. Despite this, five APs connected to the SKEGUEST network were detected, and the signal strength, along with secondary and tertiary coverage, was generally good with a minor exception for the nurse room.

The Associated Access Point view indicated only 2-3 zones for both 2.4GHz and 5.0GHz, despite the presence of five APs in the building. This could suggest some APs may be shut down or transmitting at low power levels.

No rogue devices were detected inside Building 3. However, co-channel interference (CCI) was noted from two rogue APs in a nearby building affecting AP-b9:10, and from two APs in Building 2 affecting AP-49.e0. Moreover, extensive CCI was reported across the building on the 2.4GHz band.

Packet loss was detected in two gray areas, but it is unclear whether this is a sporadic incident or due to a measurement error. This issue merits further investigation in future site surveys. The overall results suggest a need for fine-tuning of the APs and a re-evaluation once the left part of the building becomes accessible.

7.2 Site Survey Summary

The Wi-Fi network survey I conducted at Kanaskolan has given me valuable insights. Overall, the 5.0GHz network showed decent signal strength across most parts of the buildings, but I noticed weaker signals in some office spaces and classrooms that need addressing. I was able to find 25 out of the 34 Access Points (APs) on the SKEGUEST network, hinting that some APs might be in hard-to-reach areas or even in nearby buildings.

In Building 2, I found two APs really close to each other, which could cause co-channel interference (CCI). Building 3 showed some discrepancies with the number of APs versus the identified zones, making me wonder if some APs are offline or transmitting at low power.

CCI was a common issue, especially on the 2.4GHz band, and rogue APs both within and outside the buildings were major contributors. I also detected packet loss in certain 'gray' areas, which impacted both the 2.4GHz and 5.0GHz networks.

Some zones had potential for roaming oscillation, mostly in the hallways and stairs, which could interrupt smooth Wi-Fi use for people moving around the buildings. The 'sticky node' issue, where users stay connected to a distant AP, was another problem, mainly on the 2.4GHz band.

To optimize the network, we need to consider adjusting AP placements or power levels, dealing with rogue APs, improving signal coverage in weak areas, and managing CCI. Once the construction is done and all areas become accessible, a follow-up survey would be beneficial.

7.3 Site Survey Reflections

I anticipated a smooth survey, but reality had a different plan. The survey was not without its challenges, construction work in half of Building 3 barred access to several areas, making it impossible to capture complete data from the site. Further, several classrooms had been temporarily transformed into office spaces and were populated with additional APs from various companies, adding unexpected variables to the mix. Moreover, some rooms were filled to the brim with tables, chairs, and other equipment, complicating my ability to navigate and measure all parts of the room. One room was also rebuilt with new dry walls in the middle. Despite these challenges, the site survey proved to be a good learning experience.

When I returned home to analyze the results, I realized there were several things I wish I had done better. In particular, I should have taken more pictures and made more notes about the walls and the furniture in the rooms. Some walls that I had marked as thin wooden walls seemed to absorb more of the signal strength than the cement walls, which left me puzzled since cement walls should absorb much more. Therefore, I decided to revisit Kanalskolan one more time, knocking on more walls and photographing the interior of the rooms to get a better understanding of why some drywalls absorbed so much of the signal.

“ A typical interior wall that looks like drywall might have a nasty surprise inside that you don’t see. You knock on the wall, hear emptiness inside and assume 2-3dB attenuation. Then, you measure its true attenuation and reveal it is actually 12dB! Often, you will have a brick wall or sound-proofing material inside that ‘dry’ wall.”[19]

When I got home, I realized that the only explanation for why some walls, which appeared and sounded like thin wooden walls, significantly reduced the signal strength must be due to a thicker cement wall behind them, or some type of sound insulation. The only way to know for sure is to try to measure and calculate the wall attenuation on as many walls as possible.

9. Wall attenuation Calculation

When I hover with my mouse over position on the maps in Ekahau Pro, a pop-up window with stats of the measured RSSI value, from my selected AP. I used this information to print out the RSSI-values at five positions along the wall, as in Image 23,  and then print out the RSSI-values on the other side of the wall. Optimally, I would like to do the measurement 5 meters away from the AP to reduce measurement errors, but that was not possible in all rooms. Average dBm on the left side of the wall was 51,6 dBm and on the right side 59 dBm, which indicated that the wall between the classroom, absorbed around 7.4 dBM. .

I continued with selecting the AP in the other room of the same wall, and did the same calculation to confirm the same wall's attenuation. This time min calculation showed a loss of 8.6 dBm for the wall. So the average wall attenuation from both my calculations was 8 dBm.

Image 25, Signal Strength from measured AP-85.10, Building 2, Bottenplan. Printed RSSI values in red

I continued to calculate some other wall's attenuation but as seen in Image 27, I got some unexpected resultats. The left wall which I had marked as wood from knocking on it, reduced the signal strength by 8.6 dBm. And the wall on the right side of the AP, which I had marked as concrete, only reduced the signal strength by 1.8dBm.

I also selected the AP-a0.70 in the room to the right of the wall, and calculated the attenuation on the same wall and got a similar result. This wall's attenuation only reduces the signal strength with 1-2dBm, which leaves me confused since I have marked it as concrete in my notes, after knocking on it. After this I decided that I can’t trust my notes on walls too much, I need to calculate the attenuation for most of the interior walls in the school to be sure.

Image 27, Wall attenuation calculated from measured AP-a0.70, Building 2, First Floor. My walk path taking measurements is also displayed.

One other wall type that gave me trouble was the concrete wall between the classrooms and hallway which exists from the basement to the second floor in Building 2. I got a lot of different values with a range from 4.4dBm to 13.4dBm depending which classroom I did the calculations from.

Image 30, Concrete wall between the classrooms and the hallway at Building 2, basement

There could be several factors to why I got so different values for this concrete wall, as seen in image 30-31, there are a lot of metal cabinets and other obstacles that block parts of the wall in the hallway.

Image 31, concrete wall between the classrooms and the hallway at Building 2, floor one

I also realized afterwards that Iḿ not sure that the doors to the classrooms always was closed after me when I entered to take measurements of the Wi-Fi signal, there were also people working in some of the classrooms and I also walked in a zigzag pattern in the hallways when I took measurements, to get a better average RSSI of the WiFi-Signal in the hallway, as in Image 32, that was a tip I picked up from a Ekahau tutorial video on youtube.

Image 32, My walking path, taking measurements with Ekahau Sidekick

Unfortunately, I later learnt that when I want to accurately measure a specific wall attenuation, I should walk in a straight line, close to the wall, and then do the same on the other side of the wall to get more accurate RSSI values to use for wall attenuation calculation.[19]

After a lot of calculations on wall attenuation, I decided that I needed to create four different wall types to use in Ekahau Pro as seen in Image 32. There is not much of a difference between K-Concrete1 and K-Concrete2, but generally, the walls that separate the classrooms cause a little bit more attenuation even if they feel and sound like a drywall, when I knock on them. My best guess is that we have the same concrete wall all around the classrooms up to the second floor. But the concrete walls between the classroom also have thin drywalls in front of them, that's why they reduce the signal strength by 1-2dBm more than the concrete walls facing the hallways.

Image 32, Screenshot of created walls in Ekahau Pro, first column is attenuation for 2,4 GHz signal, second column is for 5.0 GHz signal and the third for 6.0 GHz signal

To measure and calculate the attenuation of the floor, it is possible to stack several maps of floors together as one building in Ekahau Pro. I then selected some different APs and tracked their RSSI on floors above or under it. My conclusion is that the floor attenuation is at 20dBm maximum.

10. Fine-tuning

Having computed wall attenuation and created new types of walls, there is an additional step to verify and refine the design, as demonstrated by my supervisor. Based on a list of all Access Points (APs) and their settings within the school, I selected the same virtual AP in Ekahau Pro, configuring it with the same settings as those on the list. I then placed it in the same position as the AP I intended to emulate. Subsequently, Ekahau was allowed to simulate radio wave propagation from the AP and generate a heatmap. If my design is accurate, the simulated heatmap should reasonably align with the measured heatmap from my site survey.

However, it's essential to acknowledge that the simulations will never exactly match reality. For instance, Ekahau Pro's simulation doesn't account for reflections in its calculations. Moreover, furniture, machinery, and other equipment present in buildings can influence the distribution of radio waves. While it's generally unproductive to attempt mapping furniture and other movable objects due to their potential for relocation over time, I've realized the value of keeping notes and photographs of rooms. Such resources can help interpret unexpected measurement results and enhance the understanding of the wireless environment.
